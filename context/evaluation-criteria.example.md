# Evaluation criteria — TaskFlow (example)

> Copy this file to `context/evaluation-criteria.md` and customize for your own standards.
> The AI judge reads this file when scoring generated articles.

## What a good TaskFlow article does

A good article:

1. **Answers the search intent directly** — The reader should have their question answered in the first 200 words, not at the end.

2. **Is honest about competitor strengths** — We acknowledge what the competitor does well before explaining where TaskFlow wins. Articles that dismiss competitors without evidence lose credibility.

3. **Explains structural differences, not just feature lists** — The gap between tools should be architectural: different data models, delivery models, or design assumptions — not just "we have X feature and they don't."

4. **Uses concrete operational outcomes** — Instead of "improves sprint planning", say "the dependency graph is resolved before sprint planning starts, so the meeting is a confirmation rather than a negotiation."

5. **Stays in TaskFlow's vocabulary** — Uses words from the glossary. Does not use competitor-branded terms to describe TaskFlow features.

6. **Has no CTAs** — Articles do not end with "book a demo" or any sales language. They end after the FAQ.

7. **Has a clear verdict** — The bottom line section names clearly who should use TaskFlow and who should not. No hedging.

8. **Is readable** — No long inline bold blocks. No walls of text. Sections are skimmable.

## Scoring weights (for the judge)

- Accuracy and honesty about the competitor: most important
- Structural depth of the argument: very important
- Clear verdict: important
- SEO / AEO optimization: important
- Brand voice compliance: important
- Format compliance (no CTAs, correct heading levels): non-negotiable

## Hard fails

- Any claim about a competitor that is false or fabricated
- Any CTA or demo offer in the article body
- FAQ answers on the same line as the ### heading
- Fewer than 5 main sections for a comparison article
